The Layer For Lara · USESI
A note from Micaila Vaughn

Lara —

You asked for my resume — here it is. I wanted to sit with what you're actually navigating before putting something in front of you. I was at SXSW last week in the AI and emerging tech sessions, and the same pattern kept surfacing: organizations investing heavily in infrastructure that misses because no one translated the actual work first.

Lighting is where that translation breaks down inside USESI.

The spec process doesn't move like the rest of the electrical portfolio. A project lives in conversations, submittals, and relationships for months before it touches a system. The decisions that drive margin happen before anyone opens a system — and right now, none of that is visible. You don't know which manufacturers delivered, performed, and negotiated their best pricing and terms. You don't know which procurement model closed most profitably. You don't know whether the rep relationship protected the deal or complicated it. That intelligence exists — it's just living in people's heads instead of anywhere it can be used. And that's only the middle of the problem.

My brother spent 20 years building and scaling complex project operations. He says it plainly: a system is only as useful as the people using it. If it hasn't been built around how they actually work, it never gets off the ground. It never flourishes.

What I'd tackle first — two immediate entry points into a much larger gap:

  • 01 A knowledge and training system built around how lighting actually gets sold, specified, and delivered — so that institutional knowledge becomes searchable and repeatable instead of walking out the door with the next retirement.
  • 02 A data layer built around the activity that's already happening — so pipeline, margin, and project status surface as a byproduct of doing the work, not as a separate reporting burden. When that's in place, leadership sees across the portfolio. Sales people see what's worth chasing. The investment already made starts to work.

That bridge — between the lighting work and the systems built to serve it — doesn't exist at USESI yet. Twenty years inside lighting — manufacturing, rep networks, regional sales. Teloric, Coronet, Luxxbox, Visual Interest, Pure Lighting. I have both sides of it.

That's the layer that's missing.

Wiedenbach Brown pioneered national account lighting in 1960 — before anyone else saw it. That instinct is still the DNA of this organization. 110 years later, the channel is shifting again. The intelligence behind every deal, every spec, every relationship now needs to operate differently.

This is a defining moment for USESI. Fourteen companies at an inflection point — where the next move determines who leads and who gets left behind.

The momentum is already here.

The only question is — are you ready to build it, or be built by it?
